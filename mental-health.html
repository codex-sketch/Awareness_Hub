Mental Health Awareness

Understanding Neurodivergence & Mental Health Support

Health

Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity. It encompasses the overall quality of life and the ability to adapt and manage physical, mental, and social challenges.

Mental Health

Mental health refers to a person's emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects how we think, feel, and act in daily life. It also determines how we handle stress, relate to others, and make decisions. Maintaining good mental health is essential for overall health and quality of life.

Neurodivergence

Neurodivergence describes the natural variations in the way people's brains function, process information, and perceive the world. It includes conditions such as Autism, ADHD, and dyslexia. These differences can influence how individuals think, learn, or respond to certain situations, but they are not flaws or choices — they are part of a person’s unique neurological makeup. Recognizing neurodivergence helps foster understanding, inclusion, and supportive environments for all.

Autism

What is Autism?

Autism, or Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects how a person perceives the world, communicates, and interacts with others. Individuals with autism may experience differences in social interaction, communication, sensory processing, and behavior patterns. Autism is a spectrum, which means its characteristics range from mild to more pronounced, and every autistic person has a unique combination of strengths and challenges.

Types of Autism

  • Autistic Disorder (Classic Autism) – noticeable challenges in communication, social interaction, and repetitive behaviors; often more severe.
  • Asperger’s Syndrome – difficulties with social interaction and restricted interests, usually with normal or above-average intelligence; milder symptoms.
  • PDD-NOS (Atypical Autism) – some but not all traits of classic autism; severity varies.
  • Childhood Disintegrative Disorder (CDD) – rare; typical development followed by significant loss of skills; more severe.
  • High-Functioning Autism (HFA) – milder symptoms with average or above-average intelligence.

Autism Spectrum Levels

  • Level 1 – Requiring Support: Mild challenges; can function with some assistance.
  • Level 2 – Requiring Substantial Support: Noticeable difficulties; needs more consistent support.
  • Level 3 – Requiring Very Substantial Support: Significant challenges; needs intensive support; more severe but less common.

24 Hours in Thomas's Life

Thomas is 11 years old, tall for his age, and autistic. Let’s walk through a day in his life and see how he experiences the world.

7:00 AM – Waking Up

Thomas wakes up to the sound of his alarm. Loud noises sometimes startle him, so he puts on his headphones. This helps him feel calm.

7:30 AM – Breakfast

At breakfast, Thomas likes his food in a specific order on the plate. If the cereal touches the toast, he gets anxious. These small routines help him feel safe.

8:30 AM – School Begins

Thomas walks into class. He’s taller than most kids, so he often stands out. When someone takes his pencil, he goes quiet. This quiet moment is his brain managing emotions. He doesn’t shout or get angry, but internally he’s working through frustration.

10:00 AM – Recess

During recess, Thomas prefers lining up the small toys instead of running around. This is how he feels safe and engaged.

12:00 PM – Lunch

Thomas sits quietly eating his sandwich. He notices the way others interact but may not join immediately. He observes first, then decides if he wants to join in.

2:00 PM – Math Class

Thomas loves patterns and numbers. When the teacher gives a tricky problem, his eyes light up. Math is one of his strengths.

3:30 PM – End of School

On the way home, Thomas prefers walking alone for a few minutes, listening to music. This helps him process everything that happened.

5:00 PM – Homework

Thomas sits at his desk with everything organized just right. If someone moves his notebook, he pauses for a second, takes a deep breath, and continues. This is part of his coping strategy.

7:00 PM – Dinner

Dinner with family is loud, but Thomas uses a quiet corner to eat comfortably. He might hum or rock slightly while eating — a self-soothing behavior. It helps him feel safe and calm.

8:30 PM – Relaxation

Thomas reads a book about space. He loves facts and visual stories. Sometimes he reads the same page twice — not because he forgot, but because it feels comforting. This is part of his sensory enjoyment.

9:30 PM – Bedtime

Thomas prepares for bed in his exact routine: pajamas, brushing teeth, arranging stuffed animals. Any change can make him anxious, so he follows his steps carefully. This helps him sleep peacefully.

ADHD

What is ADHD?

ADHD stands for Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. It is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects a person’s ability to focus, control impulses, and manage energy levels. Individuals with ADHD may find it challenging to pay attention for long periods, stay organized, or remain still when required. ADHD is often noticed in childhood but can continue into adolescence and adulthood. People with ADHD can also have unique strengths, such as creativity, hyperfocus on special interests, and high energy for tasks they enjoy.

Types of ADHD

  • Predominantly Inattentive – Difficulties with focus, organization, and following instructions. Often appears distracted or “daydreamy.”
  • Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive – Struggles with sitting still, controlling impulses, or staying calm. Often appears energetic or fidgety.
  • Combined Type – Shows both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive symptoms. Needs support for attention and activity regulation.

24 Hours in Camille's Life

Camille is 7 years old and has ADHD. Let’s explore her day and see how she experiences the world.

7:00 AM – Waking Up

Camille wakes up and immediately starts jumping on her bed. This is her natural hyperactivity in action. She sometimes forgets to brush her teeth because she’s already thinking about breakfast or school.

7:30 AM – Breakfast

While eating, Camille often gets distracted by the clock or passing cars outside. She grabs a pencil and writes down plate numbers she sees. This is a way Camille channels her attention.

8:30 AM – School Begins

Camille struggles to stay seated during lessons. She taps her pencil, shifts in her seat, or sometimes blurts out answers without raising her hand. This helps her brain process ideas in real time.

10:00 AM – Recess

Camille runs around energetically, climbs on the jungle gym, and plays tag with friends. She finds it hard to stop when the bell rings. She’s releasing excess energy.

12:00 PM – Lunch

Camille eats quickly and then starts doodling on her napkin. Sometimes she talks loudly to a friend across the table. Her mind jumps between eating, drawing, and socializing.

1:30 PM – Math Class

Camille loves hands-on activities but struggles with long instructions. If the teacher gives too many steps at once, she loses track. Breaking tasks into small steps helps her succeed.

3:00 PM – End of School

Camille bounces around on the way home, pointing out interesting things she saw during the day, like patterns on cars or birds. Her curiosity drives her energy.

4:30 PM – Homework

Camille sits at her desk, but she often gets distracted by sounds or fidgets with a toy while trying to focus. Small movements help her concentrate.

6:00 PM – Dinner

Camille chats loudly with family, sometimes switching topics rapidly. She may interrupt, but she is enthusiastic and curious about everyone’s stories. This is her excitement showing through.

7:30 PM – Relaxation

Camille draws or builds with blocks. Sometimes she hyperfocuses for a long time, forgetting the clock. It’s a positive aspect of ADHD attention.

9:00 PM – Bedtime

Camille struggles to settle down. She might run to the bathroom multiple times, or ask a lot of questions before sleep. A calming routine helps her transition to rest.
